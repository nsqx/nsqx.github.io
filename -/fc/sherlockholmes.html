Introduction
The Sherlock Holmes literary canon is one of the most famous and impactful pieces of work which changed and popularized the genre of the detective story in literature.

Sherlock Holmes is a fictional detective created by British author and physician Arthur Conan Doyle, first appearing in A Study in Scarlet, published in 1887. He displays his finesse in deduction, reasoning, and observation as he investigates each case involving various people and criminals with his friend and companion, Dr. Watson. Throughout the entirety of the original series, Dr. Watson accompanies Sherlock and narrates the scene and Sherlock’s seemingly impossible deductions.

As one of the most famous literary canons, Sherlock Holmes has impacted and popularized the detective genre in literature, remaining the most enduring of his time as he is the inspiration for numerous modern detective novels and adaptations.

Adaptations
Sherlock Holmes has also been the inspiration for numerous modern literature and adaptations, such as films and TV shows.

Many modern interpretations of Sherlock Holmes have been created since, including films and TV shows. Just like the original works, these modern visual versions also characterize and illustrate Holmes’ impeccable observation and flawless deduction. However, these modern interpretations also have several differences with the original, including aspects such as narration, emotion, and importance of characters.

Original Works Modern Interpretations

— Similarity —

Basic personality and traits of characters remain unchanged across the different versions — Sherlock remains just as sharp-eyed and cunning, making the miraculous deductions while investigating the case; and Watson is still Sherlock’s loyal friend and right-hand man, providing the help when Sherlock needs it while marvelling at his ingenuity.
Another point of similarity is the plot and story shape. They all have story and plot that fall into the same structure, where
  1. Sherlock receives a request to solve an intriguing case,
  2. Sherlock calls upon Watson for help and discusses the problem,
  3. They arrive at the crime scene,
  4. Sherlock performs his quick observations and deductions of the scene,
  5. He ensures the correctness of his guesses,
  6. – Interlude –, and finally,
  7. The criminal is caught, Sherlock is praised, wrap-up, end.
Methods of communication are the same. Sherlock remains just as sharp-eyed and cunning, making the miraculous deductions while investigating the case; and Watson is still Sherlock’s loyal friend and right-hand man, providing the help when Sherlock needs it while marvelling at his ingenuity.

— Difference —

The narrator and perspective is different between the two mediums. In the original works, the voice of the narrator is Dr. Watson, and the stories and novels are all written from his perspective, thereby concealing Holmes’ thought process, and making Holmes’ explanation more abrupt and impactful. In the modern film interpretation, the supposed “narrator” is still Watson, as revealed at the end of the movie, but Watson still appears as a character, and we are exposed to more of a 3rd-person perspective, occasionally switching to a slowed-down fight scene portraying Holmes’ thought process.
The portrayal of characters is also different. While Sherlock Holmes remains the main character in both, he acts more as a tool of narration for his deduction and thought process in the original works as the original puts more importance on the uniqueness of the case and the solution process. In the movie interpretations, Sherlock fits more into the role of the main character, as the entirety focuses on Sherlock more than the plot.
Another point of difference is the characterization. In the book, as Sherlock Holmes is only a tool of narration, he is more of a round and static character, consistently displaying intelligence and remaining the same. However, in the movies, Sherlock is more of a round and dynamic character. At the start of the (2009) one, Sherlock Holmes is having a difficult time with dealing with the fact that Watson is about to get married, and is about to leave him and his cases, and constantly disapproves. At the end of the movie, Sherlock Holmes gets better with dealing with this information. Sherlock also displays appreciation for Irene Adler, which is another point of deeper personality and developed/developing character

Overall, Sherlock Holmes shares both similarities and differences across the different mediums, as different mediums and different times focus on different things, while some things remain the same.
